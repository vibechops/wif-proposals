# Cost breakdown & income model: Google Flow × WIF India

Internal working doc. Built bottoms-up to answer one question:

> Should WIF ask Google to **pay all program costs on top of a clean WIF fee**, or should the **$100,000 / $150,000 absorb all costs** (i.e. costs come out of that number)?

To decide that, you need to see where every rupee flows, what WIF's real margin is under each structure, and what's left **after tax** (WIF is a private limited company, so we model gross income and net income, not just "fee minus spend").

FX used throughout: **$1 ≈ ₹85**. All figures are planning estimates with Low / Base / High ranges. Treat Base as the number to plan around.

> Note on the earlier doc: `pricing-recommendation.md` carried a thin top-down cost guess (Option A ≈ ₹18L, awards ≈ ₹3-8L). That was a sanity-check placeholder, not a real production budget. The model below is bottoms-up with actual crew, camera, travel and a real Mumbai awards show, so the numbers are higher and more honest. Where they differ, **use this doc for costing** and that doc for market-rate benchmarking.

## 1. Executive summary (read this first)

| | Option A | Option B |

| WIF fee (adopted) | $100,000 (₹85.0L) | $150,000 (₹127.5L) |

**The headline finding:** if the $100K / $150K are **inclusive of all costs**, Option B earns WIF almost the **same net profit as Option A (~₹19-20L)** while carrying ~70% more work, ~₹40L more cash at risk, and all the overrun risk of a live Mumbai awards show. That is a bad risk-adjusted trade.

**Recommendation in one line:** Do **not** bury costs inside the fee. Quote Google a **production budget at cost (pass-through) + a separate WIF management & curation fee**. If Google insists on a single number, Option B must be repriced up (cost-inclusive B ≈ **$175-200K**) or the awards show scaled down. Details in Section 7.

## 7. The three commercial structures (this answers your question)

### Model 1 - Fee-inclusive (proposal as written today)
Google pays $100K / $150K; WIF delivers everything within it.
- **Pro:** simplest to pitch; one clean number.
- **Con:** WIF carries 100% of cost-overrun + FX risk; Option B net is thin and can turn negative; WIF funds ₹60-100L of working capital up front.
- **Verdict:** acceptable for A, **risky and underpriced for B.**

### Model 2 - Costs pass-through + WIF fee (recommended)
Google funds the **production budget at cost** (transparent, reimbursable or milestone-paid) **plus** a separate **WIF management & curation fee** that is pure margin.
- Example A: production ₹59L (at cost) + WIF fee $60K (₹51L) → **Google pays ~₹110L (~$129K)**, WIF keeps ~$60K gross, de-risked.
- Example B: production ₹101L (at cost) + WIF fee $90-120K (₹76-102L) → **Google pays ~₹177-203L (~$208-239K)**, WIF margin protected regardless of overruns.
- **Pro:** WIF profit is locked and overrun risk sits with the budget, not WIF's margin; cleanest for a Pvt Ltd; matches how agencies bill activations.
- **Con:** bigger headline number; Google sees the cost detail (this is usually fine and builds trust).
- **Verdict:** **best risk-adjusted outcome for WIF.**

### Model 3 - Cost-plus single number (compromise)
One number = audited budget + fixed margin % (e.g. cost + 30%). Share the budget so the margin is defensible.
- Option A: ₹59.4L × 1.30 ≈ **₹77L (~$91K)**.
- Option B: ₹101L × 1.30 ≈ **₹131L (~$154K)** - note this lands almost exactly at the adopted $150K, which is why $150K only works for B if costs stay at Base.
- **Verdict:** good middle path if Google wants one number but accepts a transparent budget.

## 8. GST, TDS & how to invoice (don't leave margin on the table)

- **If Google pays from outside India (USD, to a foreign Google entity):** this is likely an **export of services**. File an **LUT** and invoice **zero-rated** - charge no GST, and **claim refund of input GST** paid on Indian costs (venues, AV, crew, hotels). This *improves* net by recovering ~18% on a large chunk of costs. **Best case - chase this.**
- **If billed to Google India (INR):** add **18% GST on top** of the fee ("fee + GST"), so WIF never eats it. Google India claims it back as input credit, so it is neutral to them. WIF also claims input GST on its costs.
- **TDS:** Google India will withhold 2% / 10%. It is **not a cost** - WIF claims it against its tax liability. Just a cash-flow timing issue.
- **Never quote a GST-inclusive lump sum by accident** - that silently hands ~18% of the fee to the government out of WIF's pocket.

**Action:** confirm the contracting Google entity (US vs India) early - it changes the effective net by lakhs.

## 9. Cash flow & working capital (the silent killer)

WIF must spend ₹60-100L **before and during** the program, often weeks before Google pays. Without milestone advances, WIF funds this from its own balance sheet.

**Recommended payment schedule:**
- 40% on signing (covers prep + first cities)
- 30% at roadshow midpoint
- 30% on delivery of final report / post-awards

For Option B specifically, ensure the **awards-show tranche is paid before the event**, not after - vendors need ~50% advances.

## 11. Bottom line / recommendation

1. **Don't absorb costs silently into the fee, especially for Option B.** At $150K cost-inclusive, B's net (~₹20L) is barely above A's and turns negative on a bad day.
2. **Pitch Model 2:** a transparent **production budget at cost + a clean WIF management & curation fee.** This protects WIF's margin and is standard for activations of this size.
3. **If Google demands one number:** keep **Option A at $100K** (fairly priced cost-inclusive), but reprice **Option B to ~$170-200K**, or strip the Mumbai awards to a leaner ₹15-18L format to make $150K work.
4. **Nail the invoicing route** (export/LUT vs "+GST") and **milestone advances** before signing - both are worth lakhs.
5. **Confirm Google covers the Flow expert's travel/lodging** in writing.

## 12. Who should handle the cash? (cost reimbursement vs Google paying direct)

A myth to kill first: **handling the expense cash does not give WIF a tax benefit.** If Google funds the costs and WIF spends them, the money in (reimbursement) and money out (expense) cancel - net profit on that portion is zero, so there is nothing to tax and nothing to save. You don't make money spending money you were handed.

What controlling the spend **actually** does for WIF:

| Lever | Real benefit? |
|---|---|
| "Tax benefit on expenses" | ❌ Myth - reimbursed cost is profit-neutral |
| GST input credit / export refund on the costs | ✅ Real cash back (~18% of eligible costs) if WIF pays vendors and invoices correctly |
| Handling / management markup on costs (if agreed) | ✅ Real margin - but only if Google agrees to it |
| Float, if costs are paid to WIF in advance | ✅ Minor, helps working capital |
| Vendor control, quality, relationships | ✅ Operational, not financial |

**Will Google say "just send us the bills / we'll pay vendors directly"?** Very likely - it's standard procurement. Three sub-cases:
- **Google pays vendors directly:** zero cash risk for WIF, but **no GST refund, no markup, no float** - WIF earns only the clean fee.
- **Reimbursable at actuals (WIF pays, bills with receipts):** WIF fronts cash, **keeps the GST refund**, earns the fee; no markup unless agreed. **Usually the best realistic outcome for WIF.**
- **Fixed production budget paid to WIF:** WIF keeps any savings as margin (and bears overruns). Best upside, but Google may resist funding a markup.

**Net:** push for **fee + costs reimbursed at actuals to WIF** (so WIF keeps the GST refund and the clean fee), with a small handling fee if you can get it. Avoid Google paying vendors directly unless you specifically want zero risk and don't mind losing the GST refund.

**How to read it:**
- **Directions 1 and 2** keep the fee (WIF's margin) clean and separate, and the cost as a transparent line. If that cost line is **reimbursable**, Google carries overrun risk, not WIF. This is the safest for WIF.
- **Direction 1** under-monetises Option B - WIF does ~70% more work for the same fee. Only choose it if simplicity matters more than money.

Use only if Google insists on a single figure - and if so, contract fixed-price with vendors to protect the margin.

> Same economics, different risk and optics. Direction 2 gives WIF the cleanest margin with the least risk; Direction 3 carries the most risk for WIF.
